ASCII

ga

Dealing with text encoding is always a lot of fun, isn't it? If you answered a big "NO", the following tip might save your ass one day.

I once stumbled upon a condition almost like this one "hérésie" == "hérésie" and had a very hard time figuring out why this code returned false.

Every time you have to handle words in foreign locales, especially when it comes from another platform, you immediately suspect something might goes wrong. And in this scenario the é was indeed the culprit.

Your eyes can't really help you to unveil the fallacy. You must inspect the ASCII code with powerful tools. Like ga

By using this shortcut for ascii on the é chars on the left hand side nothing weird was printed : <é> 233, Hex 00e9, Octal 351.

However, ga printed me another story for the ones on the right hand side: <e> 101, Hex 65, Octal 145 < > 769, Hex 0301, Octal 1401.

So while the first occurrences where legit é, the other ones where composed chars from a normal e combined with an acute accent!

To dig deeper in the unicode world, have a look at unicode.vim

ADM-3A

Bill Joy created vi using a ADM-3A terminal. Its keyboard has distinct features that heavily influenced vi design.

The ESC was located in place of the actual TAB key. Below, the CTRL key was placed instead of the CAPS-LOCK key.

Each h, j, k and l key was labelled with the arrows we know and love.

Finally, on the top right corner, the Home key was also the ~ key.

down

CTRL-d CTRL-e CTRL-f see scroll in the user-guide

The ⇟ Page Down key is often hard to reach without leaving the home row or even absent on some laptop keyboards.

To scroll the window downwards, here are a few other chords:

  • CTRL-f → down a full screen
  • CTRL-d → down a half screen
  • CTRL-e → down the number of lines you specified as count

This triad is composed of 3 consecutive letters so it's an easy way to remember them.

Guess how to go the opposite direction.

If you want to go down, you may be diving to the bottom! See how to jump to the bottom of the window and the bottom of the buffer.

home row

Did you notice the little bumps under your fingers when they rest on the f and j keys? They are tactile hints indicating the natural position of your hands on a keyboard.

The more you stay around this area, the more you optimize your hands moves which results in an increased productivity and less pain to your wrists. There's no need to reach for the mouse at the other side of your desk when quicker strategies let you teleport in the file where you need to work.

mark

m ' ` see mark-motions in the user-guide

On your mark… set… go!

Marks are handy bookmarks that can be sprinkled over your documents for recurrent access.

Technically speaking they are not much more than a saved position (line/column couple) in a given buffer.

Create a mark on a position you want go back later with m followed by a name, which is an arbitrary letter of your choice:

  • lowercase letters are marks local to the current buffer.
  • UPPERCASE letters are global ; they are saved between editing sessions.

For example you can add a V mark at the top of your .vimrc to quickly jump to it.

Then, how to jump to a specific mark? Use either ' or ` keys followed by its name.

  Second line with the 'a' mark placed on the 'p' of the word 'placed' (2,32)

Unfortunately marks are invisible! So if you don't remember where you set your them, use marks to display the list. Some plugins can also display them in the sign column, like vim-signature.

To remove marks use delmarks and give one or several victims name. If you're a less patient person, the bang version delmarks! will delete all the local ones in the current buffer. Boom, pristine again!

Special marks

While you're busy choosing the right spot to set you're hand-crafted marks, vim continuously add a few in the background. Nothing happen on screen, so your flow is not interrupted by flickering.

'' and `` are great because they correspond to the position before the latest jump. These marks are your ally to dance back and forth between two distant parts of code. Moreover, they follow the "double tap" pattern found again and again in vim.

'0 to '9, aka the number marks, let you jump back to the last positions when you last exited vim.

marks in vimL

The position of a mark can be returned by getpos("'a").

# in identifiers (vimL)

You've used the foo plugin for a long time and you really enjoy it. Well, except this little painful behavior that happens sometimes and that you would like to change.

Before bothering the maintainer with your request, you start reading its source to see if you can fix it yourself. By chance, the code is neatly factorized in functions. But they all have weird and long names, like foo#disable() or even longer foo#engine#restart().

Your intuition tells you that the # symbol acts like some kind of namespace separator. And that's right. But it has a deeper meaning.

For performance reason, evaluating too much code during vim startup will really slow down the experience. So code can be loaded on demand when execution is really needed. This mechanism is called autoload. In the runtime directory, there's a subdirectory called exactly like that: autoload. Scripts living inside will only be evaluated when requested.

foo#disable is a reflection of the file-system hierarchy: find the file called autoload/foo.vim and the function foo#disable should be inside.

The more number signs there is, the deeper you go into subdirectories.
